## Overview

Mitra is a full-stack networking platform developed in collaboration with Professor Ahmed Shammem at Western Washington University. As Lead Developer and Researcher from September 2022 through 2023, I led the design and development of a web application built to support students with autism and neurodivergent individuals — connecting them through structured mentor-mentee relationships within their community.

## Key Features

- **Mentor-Mentee Matching Algorithm**: Built a custom algorithm that pairs mentors and mentees based on shared interests, ensuring meaningful and compatible connections.
- **Real-Time Chat**: Implemented in-app messaging using Firebase to allow mentors and mentees to communicate directly within the platform.
- **User Profiles**: Designed profile pages where users can highlight their interests, background, and goals to improve match quality.
- **Community Networking**: Created a structured space for neurodivergent students to find support, build relationships, and engage with peers and mentors.

## Technologies Used

- **React.js**: For building a dynamic, component-based frontend.
- **Redux**: For managing application state across the platform.
- **Node.js**: For server-side logic and API development.
- **Firebase**: For real-time database, authentication, and chat functionality.
- **JavaScript / Babel / CSS**: For scripting, transpilation, and styling throughout the application.

## Challenges and Learnings

One of the core challenges was designing a matching system that felt personal and meaningful rather than purely algorithmic. This required thoughtful data modeling and close collaboration with supervising professors to understand the real needs of the autism community. Communicating research findings to both technical and non-technical audiences — including faculty and peers — was also a key part of the process and sharpened my ability to bridge those two worlds.

## Outcome

The platform was completed in 2023 and delivered a working networking tool tailored for neurodivergent students at Western Washington University. The project demonstrated how thoughtful software design can create inclusive digital spaces, and reinforced the value of interdisciplinary collaboration between developers, researchers, and educators.
